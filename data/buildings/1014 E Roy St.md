This compact L-shaped courtyard apartment features just about all the Anhalt trademarks you come to expect from his Tudor-Norman designs - expanses of clinker brick, steep hipped roofs with cedar shakes, half-timbering, wrought iron fixtures, projecting wooden balconies, Gothic arches, leaded and stained glass, and a picturesquely landscaped courtyard. Even though the brickwork is laid in the conventional stretcher bond, Anhalt's craftsmen used a novel technique in which 2 adjacent bricks slightly jut out at an angle and meet at a point. The slight projecting of these bricks has the quaint effect catching miniature snow drifts in the winter. This apartment was initially built with 12 units, 6 per wing, with both wings getting a separate entry hall. This configuration is similar to that of 750 Belmont in which groups of units shared entry doors rather than having 1 per unit. At least the doors were designed to be singularly charming and unique.  
  
Both this building and 1005 E Roy St right across the street were built at the same time, and both were designated Seattle Landmarks in 1978, as both are exemplary of Anhalt's style. The proximity of these two apartments and their complementary architectural style does much to make this corner of Capitol Hill particularly whimsical. Come back in 2 days to see 1005 E Roy, Anhalt's magnum opus.
