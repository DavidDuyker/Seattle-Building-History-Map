Just like Twin Gables, this is one of three buildings that Anhalt designed and developed by the Borchert Company, so it makes perfect sense that the designs of these courtyard apartments are so similar. Both of these buildings have U-shaped plans surrounding courtyards of approximately 50 by 50 square feet. These courtyard apartments both feature numerous dormers and balconies projecting from a complex roofline. And like Twin Gables, this apartment along Harvard Ave features a large gable projecting at a 45° angle from one of the inner corners of the courtyard. However, this courtyard apartment differs from the predominantly Tudor Revival design and perfectly symmetrical composition of Twin Gables. In fact, this apartment is one of Anhalt's most picturesque designs within the simple courtyard apartment archetype by masterfully combining Tudor and Norman aspects in an asymmetric yet perfectly balanced composition. While both the inner corners of Twin Gables are dominated by the two massive gables (obviously), the design approach here locates a single Tudor-inspired gable in the southwest corner and a more Norman square turret with pyramidal roof in the northwest corner. This juxtaposition is perfectly balanced in the wings outside the courtyard with a corresponding gable on the northeast corner and another square turret on the southeast corner. And both of these Tudor Revival gables feature perhaps Anhalt's finest example of brick in-filled half timbering. Throughout the rest of the apartment, other Anhalt trademarks are featured such as wood shake roofing, leaded glass windows, red brick cladding with clinker brick accents, and arched recessed entries in the courtyard.  
  
And unlike many other Anhalt apartment buildings which have eventually been converted into condos, this one is actually still rented out as apartments, with 15 charming units averaging nearly 1,000 square feet.
