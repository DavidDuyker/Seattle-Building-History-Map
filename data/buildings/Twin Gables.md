Here is another typical Anhalt courtyard apartment in Capitol Hill, this one exhibits more Tudor Revival than Norman tendencies. This is simply because it features heavier emphasis on gabled roof forms rather than hipped, and the fact that there are no square or round turrets as part of the design. Where you would usually expect to find turrets superimposed within the inner corners of the courtyard like at many other Anhalt apartments is here replaced with two prominent gables. And of course this feature is where the name "Twin Gables" comes from. These massive gables project from the corners at 45° angles and are faced in yellow clapboard and feature wide, double-height leaded glass windows. The mirroring of these gabled projections at both sides of the courtyard creates a strong sense of symmetry which is uncommon in Anhalt's otherwise picturesque designs. The two units behind these gables are the most premium of this apartment building, and are among the finest multileveled units of all Anhalt buildings.  
  
Like Oak Manor, this apartment uses the half-timbering with brick in-fill motif, but with more emphasis here, and entirely in a cross-hatched pattern. The steeply-pitched complex roofline is adorned with a variety of hipped, gabled and shed dormers of various sizes, which contribute to the overall picturesque composition. Some of the units also feature French doors opening out to projecting balconies on the second floor.  
  
Twin Gables is one of three buildings developed by the Portland-based Borchert Company, in which Anhalt was contracted as designer and builder. Again, this is one of 14 Apartments in which Edwin E. Dofsen was the official architect of the Anhalt Company. Like many other Anhalt apartment buildings, this one was aimed at the luxury market, originally having 14 large units. Eventually, some of these were subdivided into single bedroom units, so Twin Gables now consists of 16 condos.
