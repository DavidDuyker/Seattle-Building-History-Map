Near the two other Anhalt apartments in the Harvard-Belmont Historic District, here is Anhalt's final apartment to be built, but it certainly won't be the last to be featured here. Because Belmont Place was completed well into the Depression, some stinting on details is apparent here, but this apartment still retains the signature "Anhalt touch". While some of the whimsical features like oriel windows, circular turrets, and doors with cast iron hinges are omitted, the bold clinker brick façade, projecting half timbering, Gothic pointed arches, and steep roof are enough to make this Norman-Tudor design stand out. By comparing the building to the original architectural renderings for this project, it it is obvious that the plans were initially more ambitious with a larger cathedral window and treating one of the entryways as a rectangular turret with a pyramidal roof reaching far above the rest of the apartment.  
  
Another significant fact about Belmont Place is that it is Anhalt's only apartment with an internal hallway, rather than providing numerous external entrances like his numerous courtyard apartments. And unlike all the courtyard apartments, this example has a more straightforward axial orientation as opposed to the "U" and "L" shaped massings. However, the lack of a traditional courtyard is made up for by having both a front and back yard with ample wooded landscaping. In fact, Belmont Place has entrances on both Belmont Pl and Boylston Ave, so it is not readily obvious which side is the front.  
  
And like many of Anhalt's other apartment buildings, this one was eventually converted into condos.
