Over at the edge of the historic Harvard Belmont District is one of Anhalt's smallest apartment buildings at a meager 7 units. But while this may be small for an apartment building, it has been said to resemble a large English ountry manor. And what Oak Manor lacks in size, it makes up for it in the level of intricacy in its details. Anhalt himself actually lived in this carefully designed apartment himself at some point. Starting out with the already impressive suite of details introduced in his earlier apartments like Anhalt Arms, the design further elaborates on the Norman and medieval English scheme. For example, most windows are again of leaded glass, but now there are also diamond paned windows, stained glass, and even an oriel window. The term oriel is apparently derived from Anglo-Norman oriell and Late Latin oriolum, both which mean"gallery" or "porch".  
  
Half-timbering also makes an appearance with the novel use of brick slating rather than stucco filling in the spaces between the exposed timber framing. To achieve this effect, Anhalt's craftsmen started with standard 1x4x8 inch bricks with grooves down the middle, which are then broken lengthwise. These modified bricks then sometimes arranged with rough cut sides exposed for a more rustic look. In the case of Oak Manor, the bricks were creatively arranged in various geometric patterns ranging from cross-hatch, herringbone, 'X' patterns, and even a variation on the ancient Greek meander pattern. The brickwork elsewhere on the building is otherwise tame with a modest use of clinker brick throughout the façade.  
  
Due to the shape of the lot, the plan of the building is of an irregular L-shape with a triangular courtyard. And nestled at the inner angle between the wings and picturesquely juxtaposed with the half-timbered projection is circular turret with a spiral staircase.  
  
Additional intricate details of Oak Manor include inlaid mahogony entry doors, elborately forged brass keyhole lids, pegged flooring, and cedar paneled living rooms.
